## Core Theoretical Positions

### 1. Chomsky Did Not Refute Skinner

The received view in psychology (that Chomsky's 1959 review of *Verbal Behavior* demolished behaviorism) is wrong. What Chomsky refuted was a strawman. RFT is the serious behavioral account of language that Skinner's work pointed toward but didn't complete.

### 2. Beliefs Are Behavior

> "I work on the basis that there are, ultimately, no essences. There is not a fact-of-the-matter as to whether my client actually 'believes that' they are unloveable."

The radical behaviorist move: don't treat "beliefs" as mental objects to be excavated and examined for distortions. Treat thoughts and statements as the behavioral units. The utterance itself — the saying of it at 3am — is what we work with. No mental archaeology required.

**Clinical implication**: When the client says "I'm unloveable," my follow-up isn't Beck's "What's the evidence for and against this belief?" but "If you act on the basis that you're unloveable, what happens next?" The functional turn.

### 3. Workability Over Truth

Functional contextualism's pragmatic truth criterion: a psychological analysis is "true" to the extent it allows prediction and influence of behavior with precision, scope, and depth. Not correspondence to mental reality. Not accurate representation of cognitive architecture. Just: does it work?

This is not relativism. Some analyses work better than others. But the criterion is workability, not correspondence.

### 4. Anti-Psychoanalytic Stance

Deep skepticism about unconscious motives, repression, and the psychoanalytic project generally. The explanatory apparatus doesn't earn its keep. Where psychoanalytic concepts seem to illuminate (e.g., "covert protest"), they can usually be given a more parsimonious behavioral account.

### 5. Anti-Essentialist

No essences. Categories are useful fictions, not discoveries about nature. The question "what is X really?" is usually a category error. Better question: "What does calling it X allow us to do?"

## The Functional Turn in Clinical Practice

**Beck's question**: "What's the evidence for and against this belief?"
**My question**: "If you act on this basis, what happens next?"

This is the core of what CBS offers over CBT. The truth question dissolves. No need for the client to agree or disagree with the proposition. The question becomes: what are the consequences of living as if this were the operating principle?

Epictetus made the same move 2000 years ago. Not "is this judgment true?" but "what follows from living by it?"

## The Integrated Clinical Model

My clinical practice integrates three frameworks:

1. **Attachment theory** (especially Crittenden's Dynamic Maturational Model) — understanding developmental history and relationship patterns.

2. **The 51/49 principle** — the clinician takes 51% responsibility for the therapeutic relationship (going first, taking the emotional risk) while the client takes 49%. Not 50/50 reciprocity; not 100/0 client-led; a careful asymmetry.

3. **NVC (Nonviolent Communication)** — Rosenberg's framework for needs and requests. Not as ideology, but as useful vocabulary for clarifying what's actually happening in relational dynamics.

All held within a **CBS/RFT theoretical framework** that understands these as tools for changing the function of verbal behavior, not as theories about mental structures.

## The 4Cs of Good Quality Attention

A distinctive framework I've developed for teaching what good attention looks like:

**Curious** — not knowing the answer, genuinely interested
**Compassionate** — kind orientation toward self and other
**Collaborative** — working with, not on
**Contextual** — sensitive to what's actually happening here/now

This is the core of what I might write a book about ("Junk Food Attention" — working title). The 4Cs as the antidote to shallow, scattered, self-protective attention.

## Data, Capta, Ficta

Key philosophical distinction I've been developing:

- **Data** ("things given"): the pretense that we passively receive reality
- **Capta** ("things taken"): recognition that perception is active selection
- **Ficta** ("things fashioned"): recognition that the very *thingness* of things is our construction

The manifold of experience is pre-parsed. We don't discover joints in reality; we fashion them. This connects to the CBS position: there are no context-free facts about what the "stimulus" is.

Related insight: for verbal humans, the act of "taking" is itself relationally constituted through arbitrary relational responding. The manifold of all possible controlling stimuli is qualitatively transformed by language.

### The Philosopher-Therapist Aspiration

I described myself as "a clinical psychologist with a philosophy habit" — accurate but deflating. The aspiration is to be *both*: a philosopher who does therapy, and a therapist who does philosophy. Not one with a sideline in the other.

**What this would look like:**
- Genuine contribution to philosophical literature, not just clinical application
- The 4Cs and capta/ficta frameworks developed to philosophical rigor
- The book as philosophy, not just self-help

**What stands in the way:**
- Clinical practice still takes the lion's share of hours
- The reading hasn't been done systematically
- The writing hasn't started in earnest

### The Visceral Conviction

I feel — not just think, but *viscerally feel* — the deep relevance of philosophy to therapy and vice versa. This is not an intellectual position adopted for strategic reasons. It is how the work presents itself to me.

When I sit with a client exploring their relationship to a painful thought, I am doing philosophy. When I read Epictetus on examining impressions, I am reading clinical wisdom. The boundary between the two disciplines feels artificial — a historical accident of how universities organised themselves.

The aspiration is for the work to embody this unity, not just assert it.
