## The Clarinet: A Musical Road Not Taken

### Origins (Age 8)

The clarinet arrived by serendipity: an aunt had been given one as a gift, did nothing with it, and passed it to her eight-year-old nephew.

A wealthier cousin gave the family a substantial sum specifically for the musical education of the children. This funded fortnightly lessons a half-hour's drive from the Devon rectory.

### First Teacher

A master craftsman in wood and metal who built grandfather clocks from scratch — with filigree inlays and mechanisms for the tides. Also a fine clarinettist and lover of recordings of great performers. Took me through to Grade 5.

### The Ascent

- **Grade 8 with Distinction** at 15
- **LTCL (Performer's Diploma)** at 17 — a qualification most don't attempt until the end of university or music college
- **3 hours daily practice** — which didn't feel like work because I loved it

Music opened as the natural degree path since I "didn't have to work hard at it."

A local prize funded the purchase of my current pair of **Buffet RC clarinets** (A and B-flat) — the same instruments now, decades later, going to a restorer.

### The Crossroads (c. 1987)

At City University to study music, the expectation was 3+ hours daily practice. Instead, I became president of the Christian Union. My clarinet professor challenged me to choose between "going into the church" and pursuing the clarinet professionally.

I chose the church.

### The Religious Years

The clarinet found a home within faith: Christian orchestra, Mozart Clarinet Quintet with friends. Music served the larger commitment.

### The Silence After Departure

When I left the church, the clarinet lost its context. Last public performance: 2012 wedding celebration with a Gypsy Jazz band. The instruments did not leave their case for thirteen years.

### The Return (2025-2026)

A local musical family, ten minutes from home, hosts chamber music concerts. Upon learning of my musical history, they immediately offered: "Would you like to do the Mozart Quintet with us?" I said yes.

The clarinet returns — not through effortful revival, but because space is beginning to open and it senses the possibility.

## The Vipassana Experience (2017)

After years of neglecting mindful practice, watched "The Dhamma Brothers" and signed up for a 10-day silent retreat.

**The Gatekeeping**: The organisation didn't like that I already taught mindfulness. Concession: one retreat only, unless I stopped teaching mindfulness professionally.

**The Retreat**: 10-day silent retreat, UK. 11 hours daily meditation, 4am wake, Noble Silence.

**The Core Insight**: Discovering that one's mind can be very convincing about things you "have to" do or "couldn't possibly bear" — and that these are fabrications, not facts. Learning that can't be unlearned.

**Day 9**: When silence lifted, waves of urge to cry. The feeling was realising that despite the technique working, this tradition was not my path.

**Pattern Recognition**: The claim to exclusive correctness mirrors evangelical/charismatic structures I had already seen through. Any tradition that claims exclusive truth will trigger the same hostility.
